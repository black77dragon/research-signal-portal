D. Content

The core claim is model-market bifurcation.

The author argues that frontier intelligence is valuable only where upside is uncapped, such as drug discovery or hard reasoning. Routine business tasks should route to the cheapest model that clears the quality bar.

Inference cost deflation changes buyer behavior.

The article cites rapid cost declines and argues that enterprises will optimize for intelligence per dollar once AI shifts from pilot projects to scaled production workloads.

The investment angle favors installed compute owners.

If models become cheaper, smaller, and more verticalized, hyperscalers can extract more useful tokens from already-owned infrastructure, improving free cash flow and operating leverage.

The semis versus hyperscaler split depends on the capex path.

If capex falls or stabilizes, installed-base owners win and new-compute sellers may de-rate. If demand explodes and capex stays high, both can win, but the author argues hyperscalers may still surprise more positively.

H. Appendix

The accountant-versus-PhD analogy is the thesis.

Most business tasks do not need maximum intelligence. They need adequate quality at the lowest cost that meets the task requirement.

Cost deflation turns pilots into production economics.

Once companies scale AI workloads, they stop using the best model by default and start routing based on cost, quality, and task type.

The investment debate moves up the stack.

If useful output per unit of installed compute rises, hyperscalers can show better free cash flow and return on capex. That matters for MSFT, AMZN, GOOGL, and META exposure.
