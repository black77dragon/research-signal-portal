Chart Pack

No local chart pack was created. The public website images were identified as part of the article structure, but this run processed the website text and did not download or manually interpret raw chart image files.

The gradual print thesis is a baseline, not a denial of money creation.

Alden's recap separates mild-to-moderate balance-sheet expansion from crisis-scale quantitative easing. The important distinction is size relative to the starting balance sheet. This helps avoid treating every increase in central-bank liquidity as the same kind of market event.

War becomes financially important when it spreads into energy, funding, and fiscal channels.

The report does not rely only on geopolitical headlines. It maps the financial channels: energy flows through Hormuz, downstream feedstock costs, consumer pressure, import stress, treasury yields, fiscal spending, and central-bank response. That is why the report belongs in a macro dashboard.

Stagflation is the hard case for policy and portfolios.

If energy shortages push prices up while growth weakens, central banks face a conflict between inflation control and employment support. Conventional stock/bond portfolios can struggle in this setup, which is why commodities, producers, hard monies, and cash-equivalents remain relevant in Alden's framework.

The flywheel concept links deficits, conflict, and political pressure.

Alden argues that sovereign debt crises and wars often cluster because each can intensify the other. War raises spending needs; debt and financial imbalance raise political stress; political stress can create more aggressive policy choices. The practical takeaway is to watch feedback loops rather than single indicators.

The portfolio section gives a posture, not a precise trade.

The public model-portfolio update included a sale of VAL, but this A22 output does not treat that as a recommendation for Rene. The durable takeaway is broader: favor scarce and high-quality assets at reasonable valuations, keep cash-equivalents for liquidity, and avoid overreliance on long bonds in inflationary stress regimes.
