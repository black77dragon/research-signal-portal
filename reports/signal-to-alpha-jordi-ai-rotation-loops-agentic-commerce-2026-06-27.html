HDetailed Appendix For Understanding

This appendix is intentionally detailed so Rene can scan the mechanism without rewatching the full video.

The rotation is the message, not a collapse.

Jordi starts with a tough week for the S&P and QQQs, but the sector mix matters: healthcare, real estate, utilities, equal-weight breadth, and IWM held up better. His read is that capital is rotating away from high-multiple hyperscalers and crowded AI beta, not abandoning the AI thesis.

Hyperscalers are the spender side of AI.

He wants to separate the companies funding the buildout from the companies receiving the spend. Hyperscalers can have strong margins and still compress because the market is unsure whether capex turns into revenue fast enough. That is spender-side uncertainty, not proof that AI demand disappeared.

Memory remains a bottleneck, but the trade is less easy.

Micron's earnings and commentary are used as evidence that memory and storage supply shortages may take considerable time to improve. Jordi emphasizes long-term customer agreements, take-or-pay structures, automotive AI, robotics, humanoids, sovereign AI, and AI PCs as demand sources. But he also warns memory is still cyclical and violent when the cycle finally turns.

GLM 5.2 is a pressure point, not a clean replacement.

The source treats GLM 5.2 and cheaper/open-source models as a real challenge to expensive frontier pricing. The counterpoint is that private local use requires memory-rich hardware such as high-end Mac Studio configurations, which are scarce and expensive. Model price compression can coexist with hardware bottlenecks.

Loops change the token-demand equation.

Agents help with individual tasks. Loops make agents repeatedly run code review, debugging, monitoring, feedback reading, pull-request work, research, and other workflow steps. That means token demand can scale with recurring processes, not merely with the number of human users.

Loops are the bridge from no-hiring to job substitution.

Jordi's workforce point is that the first AI labor effect was companies not hiring as much. Loops create the next stage: a small number of power users can train agents to reproduce workflows that many other employees perform manually. That is where margins and job disruption become more direct.

Application-layer Sharpe ratios may improve.

He argues the bottom infrastructure layers can keep growing, but the cleanest risk/reward may shift to companies using AI to improve products, margins, and workflows. This is why he repeats Eli Lilly, healthcare, agentic commerce, and application companies rather than only semis and data centers.

Eli Lilly is an AI application example, not just a drug stock.

Jordi says he believes Eli Lilly can become the largest company in the world within five years. His mechanism is GLP-1/GLP-3 demand, obesity and addiction use cases, cash to buy IP and AI assets, internal data-center capability, and AI-enhanced drug discovery partnerships.

Stripe Sessions is the agentic-commerce roadmap.

Stripe is presented as a practical sign that AI-native businesses are moving from vibe coding to vibe deploying: agents help create, operate, deploy, monetize, and manage businesses. Jordi maps payments, checkout, financial guardrails, and agentic commerce to layer five of the AI cake.

Crypto is a strategic rail, but Bitcoin timing is not confirmed.

Jordi argues agents eventually need transaction and settlement rails, and crypto/tokenization can be that native layer. At the same time, he says Bitcoin's current chart looks poor, with a falling 200-day trend and no need to be a hero. The strategic thesis and tactical entry are separate.
