HDetailed Appendix For Understanding

This appendix is based on the email body; linked website/PDF content and inline charts were not opened.

Lower inflation is not automatically a soft landing.

The source says inflation can move lower for good or bad reasons. A benign slowdown with the Fed on hold is supportive; forced Fed hikes or recession risk are not. The dashboard should therefore track how inflation falls, not just whether it falls.

Destination indicators and path indicators answer different questions.

Gold, 10-year yields, and breakeven inflation rates help answer where inflation expectations are going. Equities, yield-curve shape, and credit spreads help answer whether the journey is easy or hard for risk assets.

The easy path extends the cycle.

In the easy path, growth slows after the first-half fiscal boost, inflation falls toward the Fed forecast, and the Fed can stay on hold. That raises the odds of extending the economic cycle and keeps market bias more constructive.

The hard path is a Fed-hike and recession-risk problem.

In the hard path, inflation stays too hot and the Fed has to hike to slow GDP growth. This increases recession probability and creates equity downside risk, especially in crowded momentum winners.

The dollar matters through financial conditions and earnings.

A stronger USD can tighten financial conditions when it reflects higher relative short rates. It can also reduce earnings growth because foreign revenues translate back into fewer dollars. 22V gives a rough rule of thumb: 1% USD strength subtracts about 25 bps from EPS growth.

Momentum risk links the USD note to recent concentration warnings.

The source says USD is historically important for Price Momentum, but the beta is unstable. The better current interpretation is: if USD strength reflects hot inflation and higher rates, the broad derisking shock would likely hit Momentum first.
