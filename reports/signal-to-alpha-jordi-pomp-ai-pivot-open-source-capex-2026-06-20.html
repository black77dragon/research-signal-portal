HDetailed Appendix For Understanding

This appendix is written to tee up Rene's next deep-dive questions, not to close them prematurely.

What shifted exactly in the AI infrastructure hypothesis?

The old hypothesis was physical scarcity: chips, memory, power, data centers, and cooling were the bottlenecks. The new complication is that models may improve through algorithmic efficiency, recursive self-improvement, mixture-of-experts, and open-source competition faster than the physical buildout arrives. That does not mean infrastructure is unnecessary. It means the marginal capex dollar needs a higher proof burden.

Is this time to get out?

No direct exit conclusion follows from the source. Jordi explicitly says there are no signs yet of capex cuts. The signal becomes an exit warning only if evidence appears: hyperscalers cut capex, memory inventory loosens, AI semiconductor stocks break trend, free cash flow gets pressured by depreciation, or model prices fall fast enough to challenge expected returns on new data centers.

Why the Chinese open-source model matters.

The GLM 5.2 / DeepSeek point is important because it attacks the assumption that the best AI must come from the largest proprietary capex budgets. If constrained Chinese labs stay close through clever architecture and efficiency, then open-source or local-model systems become a pricing and sovereignty threat to frontier labs and hyperscalers.

Why model shutdown risk changes enterprise behavior.

The Fable/Mythos discussion matters because it shows that a company relying on one frontier provider can lose access through safety, regulatory, geopolitical, or provider decisions. That increases the appeal of open-source, sovereign, local, or diversified model stacks even if closed models remain stronger for certain frontier tasks.

Why AI application companies are different from AI infrastructure companies.

Infrastructure companies sell or fund the compute stack. Application companies use intelligence inside a domain where they already own data, workflow, distribution, or scientific feedback loops. Eli Lilly is the cleanest example in the transcript because drug discovery, patient data, biology, and internal AI factories can convert intelligence into proprietary outcomes.

Why Eli Lilly matters to this thesis.

LLY is not a generic "AI software" name. The application-layer argument is that companies with valuable domain data and expensive real-world problems can turn AI into faster discovery, better targeting, and stronger operating leverage. This deserves a separate LLY deep dive because the current signal is a directional thesis update, not company-specific diligence.

Why memory risk is not the same as memory bearishness.

The transcript says memory demand can remain strong for years because companies and countries still need AI factories. The risk is timing. If capex growth decelerates or a hyperscaler pauses, memory prices and memory equities can experience an air pocket even if the long-run demand story survives.

Why Bitcoin stays separate.

Bitcoin is described as a belief and momentum asset competing for attention with AI earnings. Jordi's practical filter is technical: BTC remains weak until it gets above the 200-day moving average. That belongs in the crypto thesis, not in the AI infrastructure exit decision.
