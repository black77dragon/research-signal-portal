HDetailed Appendix For Understanding

This appendix is intentionally detailed. The paragraph headings summarize the paragraph, so Rene can scan the section quickly and still get the substance without reading the original source end to end.

High volatility is a risk signal, but not automatically a sell signal.

The email's most useful point is the distinction between discomfort and evidence. AI Goods are volatile, so the positions can move sharply and create portfolio stress. But 22V says historical volatility spikes in this group have not, by themselves, predicted weak forward relative returns. That means a drawdown should be evaluated through evidence checks rather than treated as an automatic thesis break.

The idiosyncratic worry is that cheaper AI could reduce infrastructure return on investment.

The company-specific risk is simple: if tokens become much cheaper or Chinese models deliver frontier-like performance at lower cost, investors may question whether hyperscalers and AI infrastructure companies need to spend as much. That would pressure the perceived return on investment for the buildout. 22V's view, via Dauvin Peterson, is that this risk should fade longer term because AI capex estimates are still rising.

The macro worry is that inflation could force tighter financial conditions.

The macro risk is more dangerous because it can hit many stocks at once. If inflation remains too high, the Fed may have to keep policy tight or signal renewed hikes. That can raise discount rates, increase equity risk premiums, and trigger a broad derisking. In that environment, price-momentum winners can lead to the downside because investors sell what has worked.

The 0.21% core PCE threshold turns the note into a useful monitoring rule.

22V translates the Fed's 2026 and 2027 core PCE forecasts into a monthly inflation hurdle. The short version: from June onward, monthly core PCE needs to run around 0.21% to stay on the forecast path. Above that level, the market should worry more about tightening risk. Below that level, the pressure on equity risk premiums should ease.

Regulatory relief is the short-term AI-overhang watchpoint.

The source argues that regulatory response matters because restrictions on major models can create uncertainty around frontier-model development. If restricted models return to market and OpenAI is not forced to slow releases, that removes one overhang on AI-related names. This is a policy and market-confidence signal, not a company-specific buy signal.

The practical portfolio lesson is to separate pullback opportunity from thesis deterioration.

For Rene's process, this note is most useful as a decision filter. If AI infrastructure names fall while capex revisions remain positive and inflation risk eases, the pullback may be worth research. If they fall while capex expectations roll over and financial conditions tighten, the same price decline is more dangerous. The price move alone is not enough.
